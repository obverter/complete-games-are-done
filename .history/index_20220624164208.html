Through baseball's earliest decades, the pitcher wasn't thought of as a particularly strategic role.

The pitcher's most basic goal — to throw three hittable pitches past the batter without the batter hitting the ball into play — has never changed.1

But rather than try to throw strikes past the batter, old-timey baseball pitchers were expected to just sort of fling the ball toward the batter, let him hit it somewhere, and expect that the defense would handle things.

In that way, an old-timey batter striking out was considered to be mostly a failure on their part rather than a success on the pitcher's.

And so the old-timey starting pitcher usually pitched the entire, old-timey game because their job was more or less equivalent to a modern beer-league softball pitcher:

Please just lob it over the plate, Keith, and stop trying to be fancy. We're here for a good time, not for a long time.

And when the pitcher that started the game also finished the game, the record-keepers awarded him with a Complete Game. And nobody cared. Because why should they?

As an analogy: nobody sends out an all points bulletin when you parallel park your Civic without nuking a fire hydrant, right? So it follows, then, that the old-timey pitcher shouldn't expect much or any fanfare for doing little more than not failing to meet expectations.

But this hardly ever happens now. And when it does, it's a talking point across the league for that day's news cycle.

So what changed?
