The Complete Game is Done

Once upon a time, professional baseball teams typically used one pitcher each day. So why do modern teams usually use three, four — or sometimes up to 10 — pitchers per game?

Ben Elliott @obverter
June 2022

In the game of baseball, the pitcher's most basic goal—to throw three hittable pitches past the batter without the batter hitting the ball into play—has never changed.

(If you're unfamiliar with how baseball is played, boop this footnote for a few-paragraph crash-course.1)

But during baseball's earliest decades, professional pitchers were expected to do little more than just sort of fling the ball toward the batter, let him hit it somewhere, and let his teammates playing defense take things from there.

And so the old-timey starting pitcher usually pitched the entire, old-timey game because their job was more or less equivalent to a modern beer-league softball pitcher:

Please just lob it over the plate, Keith. Don't be fancy. We're here for a good time, not for a long time.

When the pitcher that starts a baseball game also finishes that game, the record-keepers award them. with a Complete Game.

And in baseball's early years, this was a statistic that almost nobody cared about.

Because why should they?

As an analogy: nobody sends out an all points bulletin when you parallel park your Civic without nuking a fire hydrant, right? So it follows, then, that the old-timey pitcher shouldn't expect much or any fanfare for doing little more than not failing to meet expectations.

But this hardly ever happens now. And when it does, it's's a talking point across the league for that day's news cycle.

So what changed?

1 If a pitcher can throw three of these good pitches—called strikes—before they throw that same batter four bad pitches, then the batter is "out," and one of the other team's twenty-seven "outs" is exhausted.

If the pitcher throws four bad pitches, or if the batter hits the ball into play and reaches base, then that batter becomes a "runner," and the next batter comes up, and the process repeats.

Every time a runner is able to safely touch all three bases and then home plate, their team scores a run.

Every three outs, the team playing offense switches to defense, and the defense has three outs of their own with which they'll try to score.

The team that has scored the most runs after both teams have exhausted 27 outs is the winner, and everyone claps, and the losing team orders pizza and picks up the bar tab. ↩
